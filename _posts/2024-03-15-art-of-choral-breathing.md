Breath is the foundation of every note we sing. Learning to breathe well — *deeply, silently, and in unity with the ensemble* — is perhaps the most transformative skill a choral singer can develop.

## Why Breathing Matters in a Choir

Unlike solo singing, choral breathing has a communal dimension. When we breathe together, we phrase together. When we breathe differently, the ensemble frays. A unified breath creates unified sound.

> *"The phrase begins before the note does — it begins with the breath."*

## The Low Breath

Most singers, when told to "take a breath," instinctively raise their shoulders and fill only the upper lungs. This produces a tense, shallow sound. Instead, practise the **low breath**:

1. Place your hands on your lower ribs, just above the hips.
2. Inhale slowly and feel your hands push outward — not upward.
3. Your shoulders should remain completely still.
4. The belly and lower back expand; the chest stays calm.

This engages the diaphragm fully and gives you a reservoir of air that sustains long phrases.

## Staggered Breathing

For phrases that exceed a single breath, choral singing relies on **staggered breathing** — the art of breathing at different times so that the sound never stops. The rule is simple: never breathe at the same time as the person next to you. Listen, find a gap, slip away and return.

A well-practised section breathes so invisibly that the audience perceives the line as endless.

## Exercise: The Sustained Hiss

A simple daily exercise to build breath control:

- Inhale deeply (low breath, 4 counts)
- Exhale on a steady *"ssss"* for as long as possible
- Aim to extend by a second or two each day

Track your personal record. Most singers reach 30–40 seconds; trained singers reach 60+. The goal isn't the number — it's the *awareness* of your breath that this practice builds.

## In Rehearsal

At our next rehearsal, pay attention to the conductor's preparatory beat. It is a breath. Match it. Let the music begin in your body before it begins in your voice.
