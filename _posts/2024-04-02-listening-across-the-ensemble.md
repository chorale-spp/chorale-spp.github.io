Singing in a choir is, at its heart, an act of listening. The singer who listens well blends beautifully; the singer who listens only to themselves sticks out like a bright thread in silk.

## The Listening Hierarchy

Good choral listening happens on multiple levels simultaneously:

**Listen to yourself** — Is my pitch in tune? Is my vowel open? Is my dynamic in range?

**Listen to your section** — Am I blending with the voices around me, or singing *over* them?

**Listen across the choir** — Can I hear the bass line? The soprano melody? How does my part support or contrast with theirs?

The higher levels depend on the lower ones. You cannot listen outward until your own sound is settled.

## Vowel Unification

One of the most effective ways to improve choral blend is vowel unification. When singers produce the same vowel shape — the same openness of the throat, the same position of the tongue — their voices fuse naturally.

> *Listen for the vowel your neighbour is singing and match it, not yours.*

A practical approach: on any held note, close your eyes and ask "where am I in the chord?" If you hear your voice clearly above the rest, you are too loud. If you cannot hear yourself at all, you may have disappeared into the texture — which, in the right moment, is exactly right.

## Exercise: The Mirror Blend

In pairs, face each other and sing a unison pitch on a pure *"ah"* vowel. Listen until you cannot distinguish your voice from theirs. Then move a major third apart and listen to the interval ring. Adjust until the overtones shimmer.

This awareness — of resonance, of blend, of *disappearing into something larger* — is what makes choral singing unlike any other musical experience.

## A Final Thought

The conductor hears things singers cannot. Trust the instruction "blend more," even when it feels like self-effacement. The paradox of choral singing is this: *the more you give yourself to the ensemble, the more beautifully you are heard.*
